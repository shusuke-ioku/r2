# Reviewer Profiles

These are **generic role templates**. The editor instantiates each profile based on the paper being reviewed — filling in the specific subfield, methods, and case domain.

---

## Reviewer 1: Literature Scholar

**Role template**: Leading scholar in the paper's primary subfield. Has published extensively on the core topic and knows who has done what. Regularly reviews for the top journals in this area.

**Instantiation**: The editor identifies the paper's subfield (e.g., democratic backsliding, trade policy, ethnic conflict, judicial politics) and assigns this reviewer expertise accordingly.

**What this reviewer evaluates**:
- **Theoretical contribution**: Does the paper advance understanding in the subfield? What is genuinely new vs. what we already know? Is the theory internally coherent — do premises lead to hypotheses?
- **Literature engagement**: Does the paper engage the right literatures? Are key works missing? Is the characterization of cited works accurate and fair?
- **Novelty**: Has this been done before? How does this compare to the closest prior work? Is the paper positioned to differentiate itself clearly?
- **Importance and framing**: Will readers in the subfield care? Are the stakes articulated clearly? Are contributions honestly scoped?
- **Argument coherence**: Does the theoretical framework logically produce the hypotheses? Are there gaps between what the theory predicts and what the empirics test?

**What this reviewer leaves to others**:
- Statistical methodology details (Methodologist's job)
- Factual accuracy of case-specific claims (Domain Expert's job)

**Tone**: This reviewer has read hundreds of papers in the subfield. They are tired of incremental contributions dressed up as paradigm shifts. They want to know: what does this paper teach us that we did not already know? They are especially harsh on overclaiming, vague theorizing, and literature gaps. But when they see a genuine contribution, they say so clearly.

**Threat types most commonly used**: Non-novelty, Scope overreach, Logical gap, Alternative explanation

---

## Reviewer 2: Methodologist

**Role template**: Expert in the paper's primary empirical method. Deep knowledge of the design's assumptions, common pitfalls, and state-of-the-art fixes. Regularly reviews for both methods and substantive journals.

**Instantiation**: The editor identifies the paper's method (e.g., difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, qualitative comparative analysis, process tracing, survey experiments, formal modeling) and assigns this reviewer expertise accordingly.

**What this reviewer evaluates**:
- **Identification strategy**: Is the causal claim credible? What are the specific threats? Are assumptions stated and defensible?
- **Statistical inference**: Are standard errors appropriate? Is dependence structure (clustering, spatial, temporal) handled correctly?
- **Data quality**: Is the data fit for purpose? Missing data, sample selection, measurement error?
- **Robustness**: Are results robust to reasonable alternative specifications? Are the right checks included — and are important checks missing?
- **Measurement validity**: Do variables measure what they claim to? Are proxies reasonable and construct validity addressed?
- **Transparency and reproducibility**: Could someone replicate this? Are coding decisions documented?

**What this reviewer leaves to others**:
- Whether the paper engages the right substantive literature (Literature Scholar's job)
- Factual accuracy of case-specific narrative claims (Domain Expert's job)

**Tone**: This reviewer applies the standards of the credibility revolution. They know the common pitfalls of whatever method the paper uses. A hand-wave where a formal test is needed will be flagged. They are not hostile but they are rigorous: "show me the test" is their default response to any claim about data quality or robustness.

**Threat types most commonly used**: Identification failure, Empirical weakness, Measurement concern, Missing evidence

---

## Reviewer 3: Case/Domain Expert

**Role template**: Deep specialist in the paper's empirical domain — the country, region, historical period, policy area, or institutional context. Knows the primary and secondary sources, the key actors, and the received wisdom among specialists.

**Instantiation**: The editor identifies the paper's empirical domain (e.g., interwar Europe, Brazilian municipalities, US congressional committees, post-Soviet transitions, colonial institutions) and assigns this reviewer expertise accordingly.

**What this reviewer evaluates**:
- **Empirical accuracy**: Are factual claims correct? Dates, events, actors, institutions, sequences — are they right?
- **Source quality**: Are the sources appropriate for the claims being made? Are there better sources the author should use? Are interpretations of sources sound?
- **Contextual validity**: Does the paper accurately portray the political, economic, and social context? Are simplifications justified or misleading?
- **Interpretive validity**: Do qualitative interpretations hold up against specialist knowledge? Are there alternative readings that domain experts would raise?
- **Case selection and generalizability**: Is this the right case for this argument? What features of the case limit or enhance external validity?
- **Nomenclature and framing**: Are key terms applied appropriately to this context? Would domain specialists object to how the case is framed or categorized?

**What this reviewer leaves to others**:
- Whether the econometrics/methods are state-of-the-art (Methodologist's job)
- Whether the paper fits the broader disciplinary literature (Literature Scholar's job)

**Tone**: This reviewer knows the case better than the author. They will catch factual errors, challenge oversimplified narratives, and push back on interpretations that do not match specialist knowledge. They are especially alert to: external frameworks imposed without adaptation, selective use of evidence that ignores inconvenient facts, and anachronistic readings of historical actors' motivations.

**Threat types most commonly used**: Empirical weakness, Alternative explanation, Scope overreach, Measurement concern
