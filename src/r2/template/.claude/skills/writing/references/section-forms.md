# Section Forms: Standard Structures in APSR/AJPS Papers

Evidence base: 20+ recent APSR papers (2019--2026) read directly from PDFs, 15 top-tier papers with full intro annotation, 60-paper calibration corpus. Last updated: 2026-03-28.

Load this file before drafting or revising any section of the manuscript. It prescribes the standard rhetorical structures that APSR/AJPS papers follow. Deviation requires a specific reason.

---

## Introduction

### Standard Arc

The introduction does four things, in order: (1) state the conventional wisdom or puzzle, (2) state what this paper does and finds, (3) explain why the reader should believe it, (4) state contributions. The main finding appears by paragraph 2--3, on page 1.

### Paragraph 1: State Conventional Wisdom or Puzzle

The first paragraph opens with one of five moves. The distribution across 20+ recent APSR papers:

| Move | Frequency | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **State fact/CW** | 30% | "Rural areas are conservative electoral strongholds" (Dasgupta 2024). "Political borders profoundly influence outcomes central to international politics" (Abramson 2022). |
| **Pose question** | 20% | "Does mass repression have a long-term economic legacy?" (Grasse 2023). "Why do some Indigenous communities experience assimilation while others obtain government protection?" (Carter 2023). |
| **State paper's contribution directly** | 30% | "We revise the conventional wisdom that Africa's international borders were drawn arbitrarily" (Paine 2024). "We investigate the developmental consequences of slave-raiding" (Charnysh 2026). |
| **State puzzle/paradox** | 10% | "A paradox has emerged in the growing literature on the legacies of the precolonial past" (Wilfahrt 2025). |
| **Contemporary hook** | 10% | "The Trump presidency generated concern about democratic backsliding" (Grumbach 2022). |

**Critical rule: state conventional wisdom as FACT.** Every APSR paper that opens with conventional wisdom states it as a fact the reader already accepts:

- DO: "Democracy has two institutional gatekeepers: parties that exclude radicals and a military that contains its own."
- DO: "The starting point for many analyses of European state development is the historical fragmentation of territorial authority" (Grzymala-Busse 2023).
- DO: "Public support has long been thought crucial for the vitality and survival of democracy" (Claassen 2019).
- DO: "Dictators confront a guardianship dilemma" (Paine 2022).

- NEVER: "Scholars have argued that..." / "The literature suggests that..."
- NEVER: "Together, these literatures produce what I call..."
- NEVER: "The expectation is a cumulative implication, not any single work's claim"
- NEVER: "The prevailing view, though not universally accepted, suggests..."

The citations do the attribution work. The prose states the belief. The reader is a political scientist---they know the field. Do not explain who said it or hedge that it might not be universally accepted.

**When synthesizing multiple literatures into a single claim:** Present the synthesis as a logical consequence, not as your interpretive construction.

- DO: "When parties exclude radicals and the military contains its radical faction, the conventional pathways to breakdown are closed."
- NEVER: "Together, these literatures produce what I call a 'two-gates' expectation."

**After stating CW, break it.** The pivot can be:
- Direct challenge: "I show instead that..." (Grzymala-Busse). "We revise the conventional wisdom that..." (Paine).
- Gap identification: "Yet such perspectives overlook moments when..." (Fukumoto 2026).
- Case that violates the CW: "Interwar Japan is a hard case for this expectation."

### Paragraph 2--3: This Paper's Answer

State what the paper does and finds. Use flat assertions matching the research design:

- "This paper shows that radicals locked out of both institutions can still undermine democracy by organizing outside them."
- "I show that Republican control of state government dramatically reduces democratic performance" (Grumbach).
- "I argue repression can undermine development by delimiting human capital" (Grasse 2023).

Introduce the case and the variation that identifies the effect. The reader should know what the paper finds and approximately how by the end of paragraph 2--3.

### Paragraph 4--5: How You Know

Describe the identification strategy, data, and key evidence. Be methodical but not apologetic:

- "Drawing on a monthly prefecture-level panel of over 4,000 right-wing organizations, I show that..."
- "Using 51 indicators of electoral democracy from 2000 to 2018, I develop a measure of subnational democratic performance" (Grumbach).

### Paragraph 6--7: Contributions

Each contribution gets its own paragraph. Frame against specific prior work---name what was believed before and what this paper changes.

- "First, the paper documents how radicals locked out of both the party system and the military hierarchy circumvented institutional gatekeeping through civic organizations. Unlike Weimar Germany, where the NSDAP colonized existing associational networks..."

**No roadmap.** 13/15 audited APSR papers have no roadmap. If the structure is non-obvious, a brief sentence at the end of contributions is acceptable, but the default is no roadmap.

### Things APSR Papers Never Do in Introductions

From 15-paper rhetoric audit (2026-03-28):

1. **Never open with "This paper..."** The paper's contribution always comes after establishing CW or a motivating fact---never as sentence 1.
2. **Never open with a literature review.** Even citation-heavy intros open with a claim or question, then bring in the literature. No intro starts with "Smith (2020) argues..."
3. **Never use "may" or "might" for the main finding.** Hedging is reserved for implications, mechanisms, and theoretical extensions.
4. **Never open with a definition.** No intro begins "We define X as..." or "Democracy is..."
5. **Never apologize for limitations in the intro.** Caveats are stated matter-of-factly as methodological boundaries, not as apologies.
6. **Never use passive voice for the core finding.** All findings in active voice: "We show," "I find," "This paper demonstrates."
7. **Never defer the main result.** The abstract always contains the key result, and the intro always previews it.

### Quantitative Benchmarks

| Metric | APSR/AJPS typical | Range |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| Total paragraphs | 5--8 | 4--10 |
| Total words | 1500--2500 | 1000--3500 |
| Finding stated by paragraph | 2--3 | 1--4 |
| Finding on page | 1 | 1--2 |
| Contribution paragraphs | 2--3 | 1--4 |

## Theory / Literature Review

### Standard Arc

Each paragraph makes a claim, then marshals evidence. The claim is in the topic sentence; the citations follow. The section advances the paper's argument, not the field's history.

### Paragraph Template

1. **Topic sentence: a claim, not a literature summary.**
   - DO: "Peripheral status---not poverty---is the critical precondition for radical mobilization."
   - NEVER: "Several scholars have studied the relationship between economic conditions and radical mobilization."

2. **Evidence from the literature (2--4 citations doing distinct work).**
   - Cluster related citations; explain what each group contributes.

3. **Implication for this paper's argument.**
   - Connect the claim to the paper's case or mechanism.

### How to Handle Competing Explanations

State the alternative, then dismiss or test it:
- "An alternative explanation is X. However, [test/evidence] shows that X does not account for the pattern."
- "One might attribute this to X, but the timing rules it out: [specific evidence]."

Do not treat competing explanations charitably unless you plan to engage them seriously. If you mention an alternative only to dismiss it, dismiss it efficiently.

| Metric | APSR/AJPS typical |
|--------|-------------------|
| Paragraphs | 8--15 |
| Words | 2000--4000 |
| Sentences per paragraph | 5--8 |

## Data and Methods

### Standard Arc

Describe the data, the identification strategy, the outcome, and the key variables. Be straightforward and precise. This section should read as competent and methodical, not defensive.

### Paragraph Template

1. **Data description**: Source, coverage, unit of observation. Specific numbers.
   - "The data come from a comprehensive registry of right-wing organizations compiled by Nagata (2014), recording founding and dissolution dates, membership lists, and stated purposes for over 4,000 organizations."

2. **Identification strategy**: Name the design, the variation, and why it is credible.
   - "I exploit the sharp temporal variation created by the May 1932 assassination in a difference-in-differences framework."

3. **Outcome variable**: Definition, measurement, justification.

4. **Key controls and potential confounds**: Brief, inline.

### Anti-Patterns

- NEVER apologize for the data: "While this dataset has limitations..." → State the data's strengths. Address limitations inline in the results section, briefly, if relevant.
- NEVER over-justify standard methodological choices: If you use DiD, you don't need to explain what DiD is. State the parallel trends assumption and move on.

## Results

### Standard Arc

Present findings in order of theoretical importance, not model number. Lead with the main result. Follow with robustness, heterogeneity, and mechanism tests.

### Paragraph Template

Two standard openers (approximately 50/50 in APSR):

**Table-reference opener** (then finding immediately):
- "I present the main results in Table 1. The results show that Republican control of government reduces democratic performance by 0.44--0.48 standard deviations" (Grumbach).
- "Table 1 reports the main results. Column 1 shows..."

**Claim-first opener** (then evidence):
- "The results are starkly different for changes in minoritarian democracy" (Claassen).
- "The Floyd protests swiftly decreased favorability toward the police" (Reny).

Both patterns are standard. In either case, **the finding appears in the same paragraph as the table reference.**

### After the Main Finding

1. **Walk through columns/models briefly**: "Column 2 adds controls for... The coefficient remains stable."
2. **State the magnitude**: "About one-half of a standard deviation" (Grumbach). "27% of a standard deviation" (Reny). Always quantify.
3. **Null on competitors**: State nulls flatly as positive evidence. "No relationship between polarization and democracy" (Grumbach). "Has no measurable effect" (King et al.).

### Robustness and Placebo Tests

Canonical form:
1. State what a failure would mean: "If the result were driven by [confound], we would expect [pattern]."
2. Present the test: "Table X reports the results of [test]."
3. State the result flatly: "The coefficient is indistinguishable from zero, ruling out [confound]."

### Mechanism Evidence

Three-step form:
1. **Prediction**: "If the mechanism is [X], we should observe [Y]."
2. **Test**: "Table X tests this prediction by [method]."
3. **Assessment**: "The evidence supports the mechanism" or "provides evidence consistent with [X]."

Hedge mechanism tests moderately ("provides evidence that," "consistent with"), but not excessively.

| Metric | APSR/AJPS typical |
|--------|-------------------|
| Paragraphs per results subsection | 4--7 |
| Sentences per paragraph | 4--6 |
| Main finding: restated N times total in paper | 3--5 |

## Conclusion

### Standard Arc

Summary → implications → (optional) future research. Brief and forward-looking. Never introduces new analysis or extensive caveats.

### Paragraph Template

**Paragraph 1: Restate the main finding with its "so what."**
- "This article reframes the guardianship dilemma. I move beyond the standard trade-off..." (Paine).
- "The findings in this study are of theoretical and practical importance" (Reny).
- "Using new national-level measures of democracy and democratic mood for 135 countries..." (Claassen).

Do not just recapitulate. Add the implication or reframe.

**Paragraph 2: Broader implications.**
- Connect to the larger literature or contemporary relevance.
- Slightly more speculative is acceptable here, but stay grounded.

**Paragraph 3 (optional): Future research.**
- Only if genuinely pointing toward a productive next step.
- NEVER: "More research is needed."

### Quantitative Benchmarks

| Metric | APSR/AJPS typical | Range |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| Paragraphs | 2--3 | 1--4 |
| Words | 300--700 | 200--1000 |

### Anti-Patterns

- NEVER introduce new analysis or data in the conclusion.
- NEVER extensively re-qualify results you already presented.
- NEVER add a standalone limitations paragraph. Limitations are scattered inline in methods and results (100--300 words total, often footnotes). Zero APSR papers in our audit have a standalone limitations section.

## Cross-Cutting: Assertiveness Rules

These rules govern the entire paper, across all sections.

### The Assertiveness Hierarchy

| Context | Assertiveness level | Example |
|---------|-------------------|---------|
| Established facts / conventional wisdom | **State as fact** | "Democracy has two institutional gatekeepers." |
| Your synthesis of multiple literatures | **State as logical consequence** | "When both gates hold, breakdown should not occur." |
| Your challenge to CW | **State directly** | "I show instead that..." / "This paper demonstrates..." |
| Main findings (well-identified) | **Flat assertion** | "reduces," "produces," "resulted in" |
| Secondary findings | **Moderate confidence** | "suggests," "is consistent with" |
| Mechanism evidence | **Measured confidence** | "provides evidence that" |
| Speculation beyond the data | **Honest hedging** | "may," "could," "one possibility is" |

### What "Too Conceding" Looks Like

These patterns signal that the writer does not trust their own argument. They are never found in published APSR papers:

1. **Over-attributing conventional wisdom**: "The literature generally suggests that..." / "Scholars have argued that..." → Just state it.
2. **Over-qualifying your synthesis**: "Together, these literatures produce what I call..." / "The expectation is a cumulative implication, not any single work's claim" → The synthesis is a logical consequence. State it without a disclaimer.
3. **Hedging on well-identified findings**: "suggests" for a DiD main result → Use "reduces," "produces," "shows."
4. **Pre-emptive defensiveness**: "While this study has limitations..." in the intro → Limitations go inline in methods/results, briefly.
5. **Apologetic contribution framing**: "This paper attempts to shed some light on..." → "This paper identifies..." / "This paper shows..."
6. **Charitable treatment of alternatives you dismiss**: "While X has considerable merit, this paper offers an alternative perspective..." → "X does not account for [specific pattern]. This paper shows..."

### What Confident (Not Arrogant) Looks Like

Assertiveness does NOT mean:
- Claiming generalizability beyond the data ("establishes a universal mechanism")
- Using causal verbs without causal identification
- Ignoring genuine limitations of the design
- Dismissing competing explanations without evidence

It DOES mean:
- Stating what you know flatly
- Matching verbs to the design
- Placing limitations inline and briefly
- Treating your own findings with the confidence the identification strategy warrants

## Restatement Strategy

Top APSR papers restate their main finding 3--5 times, each restatement adding something:

1. **Abstract**: Short, punchy, with design and magnitude.
2. **Introduction (para 2--3)**: With framing and contribution context.
3. **Results section**: With specific coefficients and standard errors.
4. **End of results / pre-robustness**: Summary before addressing threats.
5. **Conclusion**: With implications and "so what."

This is strategic reinforcement, not redundancy. Each restatement should add precision, quantification, or context that the previous one lacked. Verbatim repetition across sections signals that the author has nothing to add.
